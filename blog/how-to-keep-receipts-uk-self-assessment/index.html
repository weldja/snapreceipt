How to Keep Receipts for UK Self Assessment (2025–26)

📅 Updated April 2026 ⏱ 7 min read 🇬🇧 UK freelancers & sole traders

If you're a UK freelancer or sole trader, keeping receipts is not optional — it's a legal requirement under HMRC rules. Get it wrong and you could face penalties, an investigation, or simply lose out on tax deductions you're entitled to.

The good news: keeping records has never been easier. This guide covers exactly what HMRC expects, which receipts you need to keep, for how long, and the fastest way to stay organised throughout the year so January never catches you off guard again.

Why keeping receipts matters for Self Assessment

When you complete your Self Assessment tax return, you're claiming allowable business expenses to reduce your taxable income. HMRC doesn't ask to see your receipts when you file — but they absolutely can ask for them during an enquiry or investigation.

If you can't produce evidence of a claimed expense, HMRC can disallow it. That means you pay more tax, plus potential interest and penalties. Keeping clean records protects you and ensures you claim everything you're entitled to.

What HMRC requires you to keep

Under UK tax law, self-employed individuals must keep records of:

  • All sales and business income
  • All business purchases and expenses
  • Any assets bought for use in the business
  • Personal income (if relevant to your return)

For each business expense, you should ideally have a receipt or invoice showing:

  • The supplier's name and address
  • The date of the transaction
  • The amount paid (including VAT if applicable)
  • A description of what was purchased

Important: Bank statements alone are not sufficient evidence. HMRC expects itemised receipts for business expenses. A transaction on your statement that says "AMAZON" tells them nothing about what was purchased or whether it was a legitimate business expense.

How long must you keep receipts?

HMRC requires self-employed individuals to keep business records for at least 5 years after the 31 January submission deadline of the relevant tax year. In practice, this means most records need to be kept for around 6 years.

If HMRC suspects serious fraud, they can go back up to 20 years — though this is rare and targeted at deliberate evasion rather than honest mistakes.

Do paper receipts count? Can I use digital copies?

Yes — HMRC accepts digital copies of receipts. You do not need to keep the physical paper receipt as long as you have a clear, legible digital copy. A photograph or scan of a receipt is acceptable.

This is especially useful because paper receipts fade, get lost in pockets and bags, and deteriorate over time. A digital record stored securely in the cloud is far more reliable than a shoebox.

Pro tip: Get into the habit of photographing receipts the moment you get them — before they go in your bag, wallet or pocket. It takes five seconds and means you'll never lose one again.

What counts as an allowable business expense?

The general rule is that an expense must be "wholly and exclusively" for the purpose of your business. Common allowable expenses for UK freelancers include:

  • Office costs — stationery, printing, postage, software subscriptions
  • Travel — train, bus, fuel for business journeys (not commuting)
  • Equipment — computers, phones, cameras used for work
  • Professional fees — accountant fees, legal advice
  • Marketing — advertising, website costs, business cards
  • Clothing — uniforms or protective clothing (not general business attire)
  • Training — courses and books directly related to your current work
  • Use of home as office — a proportion of home costs if you work from home
  • Meals — subsistence costs when working away from your usual place of work

When in doubt, keep the receipt anyway. Your accountant can decide at year end whether something qualifies.

The worst receipt-keeping mistakes freelancers make

1. Leaving it all to January

The classic mistake. Twelve months of receipts stuffed in a drawer, a shoebox, or lost entirely. Trying to reconstruct your expenses in January is stressful, inaccurate, and means you almost certainly miss legitimate deductions. Log receipts as you get them.

2. Relying only on bank statements

Bank and card statements are useful for cross-referencing, but they don't prove what the expense was for. Keep the itemised receipt.

3. Mixing personal and business expenses

Use a dedicated business bank account if possible. It makes your records cleaner and makes HMRC enquiries far less stressful.

4. Not keeping receipts for small purchases

Small expenses add up. A £3.50 coffee at a client meeting, a £12 parking charge — these are all legitimate business expenses if properly recorded. A year of small receipts can easily amount to hundreds of pounds of deductions.

5. Losing VAT receipts

If you're VAT-registered, you need a valid VAT receipt to reclaim input VAT. A card statement or bank entry is not enough — you need the actual VAT invoice.

Making Tax Digital — what changes for receipt-keeping

Making Tax Digital for Income Tax Self Assessment (MTD ITSA) is HMRC's initiative to move tax records fully digital. Under MTD ITSA, affected taxpayers must:

  • Keep digital records of all income and expenses
  • Submit quarterly updates to HMRC via MTD-compatible software
  • Submit a final end-of-year declaration

MTD ITSA currently applies to sole traders and landlords with combined income over £50,000 (from April 2026). The threshold drops to £30,000 in April 2027 and £20,000 in April 2028.

Summary: what you need to know

  • HMRC requires self-employed people to keep records of all business income and expenses

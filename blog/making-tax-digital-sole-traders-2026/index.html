Making Tax Digital for sole traders (2026): plain-English guide to what's changed

April 2026 · 11 min read · By SnapReceipt

Making Tax Digital for Income Tax (MTD ITSA) went live on 6 April 2026. If you're a UK sole trader or freelancer earning over £50,000, the way you report your income to HMRC has fundamentally changed. If you're under that threshold, it's coming for you in 2027.

This guide cuts through the jargon. It covers who is affected now, exactly what changes, the quarterly deadlines you need to know, and what you actually need to do — explained for people who run businesses, not accountants.

📌 Quick answer: MTD doesn't change when you pay tax. It changes how often you report income and expenses to HMRC — from once a year to four times a year, plus a final declaration. Your January payment deadline stays the same.

What is Making Tax Digital for Income Tax?

Making Tax Digital (MTD) is HMRC's programme to replace paper and manual tax reporting with digital record-keeping and regular electronic submissions. MTD for VAT has been in place since 2019. MTD for Income Tax — which affects sole traders and landlords — started on 6 April 2026.

Under the old system, you kept records however you liked (a spreadsheet, a shoebox, accounting software) and filed one Self Assessment tax return per year, usually in January. Under MTD, you must:

  • Keep digital records of all income and expenses as they happen
  • Submit a quarterly update to HMRC four times per tax year
  • Submit a final declaration (replacing the old Self Assessment return) after the year ends

The quarterly updates are not tax returns — they're summaries. You're telling HMRC your running income and expense totals. You don't pay tax quarterly; the annual payment dates stay the same.

Whether and when MTD applies to you depends on your qualifying income — your total gross income from self-employment and/or property before expenses.

What counts as qualifying income?

Qualifying income is your gross (before expenses) income from:

  • Self-employment / freelance work
  • Rental income from property

It does not include PAYE employment income, dividends, savings interest, or pension income. HMRC uses your income from the previous tax year to determine whether MTD applies.

What changes under MTD — and what stays the same?

Before MTD (Self Assessment)
📅One tax return per year, filed by 31 January
📦Records kept however you liked — paper, spreadsheet, software
🗓Five submissions to HMRC per year (return + 2 payments on account + balancing payment + NI)
💳Tax paid 31 January and 31 July
🖥Could file directly on HMRC website
Under MTD (from April 2026)
📅Four quarterly updates + one final declaration per year
💻Digital records required — must use compatible software
🗓Nine submissions per year (4 quarterly + final declaration + 2 POA + balancing payment + NI)
💳Tax payment dates unchanged — still 31 January and 31 July
🖥Cannot use HMRC website — must use approved software

What stays exactly the same

  • When you pay tax — still 31 January and 31 July
  • How much tax you pay — same rates, same allowances, same allowable expenses
  • The personal allowance (£12,570)
  • Payments on account rules
  • The deadline for your final declaration — 31 January after the tax year ends

✅ A silver lining: Because you're summarising income and expenses quarterly, HMRC can show you a running tax estimate throughout the year. No more January surprises — you can see roughly what you owe as you go and budget accordingly.

Your income tax is still due on 31 January 2027 (for the 2025/26 tax year) and 31 July 2027 (second payment on account). MTD does not move these dates.

Option 2: Spreadsheets + bridging software

If you want to keep using a spreadsheet for your records, you can — but you'll need bridging software to connect it to HMRC's systems for the quarterly submissions. Spreadsheets alone are not MTD-compliant.

Bridging software options include 123 Sheets (~£20/year) and TaxNav (~£5/month). This route works but adds a step — you export your spreadsheet data, import it into the bridging software, and submit from there.

💡 Check the official list: Before committing to any software, verify it's on HMRC's approved MTD software list. The list is updated regularly as new providers complete testing.

What digital records does HMRC require under MTD?

MTD requires you to keep digital records of:

  • All business income — invoices, payments received, date and amount
  • All allowable business expenses — with date, supplier, amount and category
  • Mileage records if claiming vehicle expenses — date, route, purpose and miles
  • Property income and expenses if you're a landlord

You do not need to keep digital records of:

  • PAYE employment income (handled by your employer)
  • Savings interest or dividend income
  • Personal expenditure

The records must be kept in a digital format and flow into your quarterly submissions without manual re-keying. Scanning receipts immediately as you spend — rather than trying to reconstruct records at the end of each quarter — is far more reliable in practice. MTD and mileage — what you need to log digitally

If you claim business mileage (45p per mile for the first 10,000 miles, 25p after for cars and vans), your mileage records must now be kept digitally under MTD. A paper logbook is no longer sufficient if you're within the MTD threshold.

Each journey record should include:

  • Date of the journey
  • Start and end location
  • Business purpose
  • Number of miles driven

Log each trip immediately after you drive it — reconstructing mileage from memory at the end of a quarter is exactly the kind of estimate HMRC will query. See our complete guide to HMRC allowable expenses for more on vehicle costs.

What happens if I miss an MTD quarterly update deadline?

Under the new points-based penalty system, each missed quarterly submission earns a penalty point. Accumulate enough points and a financial penalty follows. However, HMRC has confirmed a soft landing for 2026/27: no penalty points will be issued for late quarterly submissions during the first year.

From 2027/28 onwards, the full points system applies:

Points accumulatedConsequence
1–3 pointsNo financial penalty — warning territory
4 points (threshold)£200 financial penalty
Each further late submission at thresholdAdditional £200 penalty
Points reset after24 months of full compliance

⚠️ Late payment penalties still apply in 2026/27. The soft landing only covers late submissions. If you underpay tax by the 31 January or 31 July deadlines, the usual late payment surcharges and interest apply immediately. See our full guide to Self Assessment penalties →

How to get MTD-ready: a practical checklist

  • Check if MTD applies to you now — qualifying income over £50,000 from 6 April 2026, over £30,000 from April 2027
  • Choose your MTD-compatible software — Xero, FreeAgent, QuickBooks, Sage, or bridging software if you prefer spreadsheets
  • Sign up for MTD with HMRC — done through your HMRC online account or via your accounting software. Do this before your first quarterly submission deadline (7 August 2026 for Q1)
  • Start keeping digital records from 6 April 2026 — scan receipts immediately, log mileage digitally, record all income as it arrives
  • Set calendar reminders for all four quarterly deadlines (7 Aug, 7 Nov, 7 Feb, 7 May)
  • Tell your accountant if you use one — they may need to update how they work with your records

✅ If you're below £50,000 this year: You're not required to use MTD yet — but the £30,000 threshold arrives in April 2027. Starting digital record-keeping now means zero disruption when it becomes mandatory for you.

Frequently asked questions about Making Tax Digital for sole traders

Do I need to use Making Tax Digital as a sole trader?

From 6 April 2026, MTD for Income Tax is mandatory for sole traders and landlords with qualifying income (gross self-employment and/or property income) over £50,000. The threshold drops to £30,000 from April 2027. If your qualifying income is below £30,000, MTD does not currently apply — but HMRC has indicated it will be extended further in future.

What counts as qualifying income for Making Tax Digital?

Qualifying income is your total gross income from self-employment and/or property before deducting expenses. PAYE employment income, dividends, and savings interest do not count. If you have multiple income streams — for example £35,000 freelance and £20,000 rental — you add them: £55,000 would bring you within MTD from April 2026.

What are the MTD quarterly update deadlines for 2026/27?

The four deadlines for the 2026/27 tax year are: 7 August 2026 (Q1), 7 November 2026 (Q2), 7 February 2027 (Q3), and 7 May 2027 (Q4). The final declaration replacing your annual tax return is due by 31 January 2028.

Do my tax payment dates change under Making Tax Digital?

No. Your income tax payment deadlines are unchanged: 31 January for your balancing payment and first payment on account, and 31 July for your second payment on account. MTD changes how and how often you report income and expenses — not when you pay.

Can I still use a spreadsheet under Making Tax Digital?

Yes, but only with bridging software that connects your spreadsheet to HMRC's systems. You cannot manually type quarterly totals into HMRC's website under MTD. Bridging software options include 123 Sheets (~£20/year) and TaxNav. For most sole traders, all-in-one accounting software is simpler.

What happens if I miss an MTD quarterly update deadline?

HMRC has confirmed a soft landing for 2026/27 — no penalty points for late quarterly submissions in the first year. Late payment penalties still apply. From 2027/28, a points-based system applies: reach 4 points and a £200 financial penalty follows. Points reset after 24 months of full compliance.

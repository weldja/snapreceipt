How to track business expenses for UK Self Assessment (2026)

April 2026 · 10 min read · By SnapReceipt

Every January, thousands of UK freelancers and sole traders go through the same ritual: hunting through a shoebox of crumpled receipts, scrolling through months of bank statements, and trying to remember what that £43 payment to Amazon was actually for.

It doesn't have to be like this. With a simple system you can stick to throughout the year, tracking business expenses for UK Self Assessment becomes a 30-minute job instead of a two-day ordeal.

This guide covers everything you need to know — what counts as a business expense for HMRC Self Assessment, how to keep records that satisfy HMRC, and the easiest way to stay on top of it all without spending hours on admin.

What counts as a business expense for UK Self Assessment?

HMRC allows you to deduct costs that are incurred "wholly and exclusively" for business purposes. In plain English: if you spent the money because of your work, you can probably claim it.

Common deductible expenses for freelancers and sole traders include:

  • Travel — fuel, train tickets, parking, business mileage (not commuting to a regular workplace)
  • Equipment — laptops, phones, cameras, tools — anything you use for work
  • Software and subscriptions — accounting tools, design software, cloud storage, project management apps
  • Office costs — stationery, printer ink, postage, a proportion of your home broadband if you work from home
  • Professional fees — accountants, solicitors, professional memberships, trade subscriptions
  • Marketing — website costs, advertising, business cards
  • Meals with clients — meals where the primary purpose is a business meeting (keep notes on who you met and why)

💡 The golden rule: If an expense is partly personal and partly business — like a phone you use for both — you can only claim the business portion. HMRC expects a reasonable, documented estimate. See our full guide: what expenses can a UK sole trader claim.

HMRC record-keeping rules for sole traders (2026)

As a sole trader, HMRC requires you to keep records of all your business income and expenses for at least 5 years after the Self Assessment deadline for that tax year. For the 2025/26 tax year (ending 5 April 2026), you need to keep records until at least 31 January 2032.

How long must you keep business receipts for HMRC?

The 5-year rule is the minimum. In practice, HMRC can open an enquiry into your tax return up to 4 years after the filing date for minor errors, and up to 20 years if it suspects deliberate non-compliance. Keeping digital records indefinitely costs nothing — there is no reason to delete them.

Your records should include:

  • All receipts and invoices for business purchases
  • Bank statements showing business transactions
  • Records of any cash transactions
  • Mileage logs if you're claiming business travel

Can you keep digital receipts instead of paper?

Yes. HMRC accepts digital records — you do not need to keep paper receipts if you have a clear digital copy showing the date, supplier, amount and VAT. This is exactly why scanning receipts as you go is so much better than keeping a shoebox.

📋 From April 2026 — Making Tax Digital (MTD) for Income Tax: Sole traders with income over £50,000 must now keep digital records and submit quarterly updates to HMRC under MTD for Income Tax Self Assessment (MTD ITSA). If you're below £50k, digital record-keeping is still strongly recommended — the MTD threshold drops to £30,000 in April 2027. See HMRC's MTD guidance →

💡 Always add a note explaining the business purpose — especially for meals and entertainment. "Lunch with client" is not enough; "Lunch with Sarah Jones from Acme Ltd to discuss Q2 project" is much better if HMRC ever asks.

How to track business mileage for HMRC

Mileage is one of the most commonly missed expense categories. If you use your own car for business journeys — visiting clients, travelling to temporary workplaces, attending training — you can claim the HMRC-approved mileage rate rather than actual fuel costs.

For motorcycles the rate is 24p per mile, and for bicycles it is 20p per mile.

What your mileage log must include: For each journey, record the date, start and end point, the business purpose, and the number of miles driven. A simple note on your phone or a dedicated mileage app works well. HMRC can — and does — reject mileage claims without a contemporaneous log.

📊 Worked example: You drive 8,000 business miles in 2025/26. Your mileage claim is 8,000 × 45p = £3,600 deducted from your taxable profit. At 20% basic rate tax, that saves you £720 in Income Tax.

Note: if you choose to claim actual vehicle costs (fuel, insurance, servicing) instead of mileage rates, you cannot also claim mileage rates. Most sole traders find the approved mileage rate simpler and more generous.

Why most freelancer expense tracking systems fail

Most freelancers fall into one of two traps:

The shoebox approach: Keep all receipts in a drawer or bag, then sort them at tax return time. This works until you lose a receipt, can't read a faded thermal printout, or forget what something was for six months later.

The spreadsheet approach: Manually type every receipt into a spreadsheet. Accurate, but time-consuming enough that most people fall behind and end up doing it in bulk — which is the same problem as the shoebox, just with extra steps.

The fundamental issue with both is that they rely on doing admin at a time that isn't the moment the expense happens. The further you get from the point of purchase, the harder it is to remember the details.

How to track business expenses throughout the year — step by step

The most effective system is one you actually use consistently. That means it needs to be fast enough to do on the spot — at the restaurant, at the petrol station, leaving a client meeting.

  1. Scan or photograph every receipt immediately — before it goes in your pocket or bag. This is the critical step. Everything else follows from it.
  2. Categorise it on the spot — Travel, Meals, Software etc. Takes two seconds and makes your quarterly export clean.
  3. Add a one-line note if it's not obvious — "Client lunch — Sarah Jones" or "Broadband — 50% business use". You'll be glad you did in January.
  4. Log mileage immediately after each journey — date, destination, purpose, miles. Don't try to reconstruct this from memory later.
  5. Export to CSV at the end of each quarter — send to your accountant or keep for your Self Assessment return. Four times a year takes 5 minutes each time.

That's the whole system. The maths are compelling: 2 minutes scanning receipts after each purchase, 5 purchases per week = 10 minutes of admin per week. Over a tax year, that's roughly 8 hours of total admin — versus the 2-day January scramble most freelancers experience.

What to do with receipts you've already missed

If you're starting mid-year with a backlog, don't panic. Work backwards through your bank and credit card statements — most business expenses will be visible there. For any transactions you can't match to a receipt, check your email inbox for digital receipts and order confirmations.

Going forward, the key is consistency. Even capturing 80% of your receipts is vastly better than 0%, and once the habit is established it becomes automatic.

VAT for sole traders: do you need to track it?

If your taxable turnover is below the VAT registration threshold (£90,000 as of 2026), you don't need to register for VAT and don't charge it to clients. However, noting the VAT shown on your receipts is useful context — some accountants want it, and it's required if you approach the threshold.

If you are VAT-registered, you'll need to keep a VAT account and file quarterly VAT returns separately from your Self Assessment. In this case, accounting software rather than a manual CSV is worth the investment.

Frequently asked questions about tracking expenses for Self Assessment

How long do I need to keep business receipts for HMRC?

HMRC requires sole traders to keep business records for at least 5 years after the 31 January Self Assessment deadline for that tax year. For the 2025/26 tax year, that means keeping records until at least 31 January 2032. Keeping digital records costs nothing, so there's no reason to delete them earlier.

Can I use digital receipts instead of paper for HMRC Self Assessment?

Yes. HMRC accepts digital copies of receipts, provided the image is clear and shows the date, supplier, amount and VAT breakdown. From April 2026, Making Tax Digital requires digital record-keeping for sole traders earning over £50,000.

What is the easiest way to track business expenses as a UK freelancer?

Scanning receipts immediately at the point of purchase — before they go in your pocket — is the single most effective habit. Using an app that reads the details automatically means the whole thing takes under 30 seconds per receipt.

Do I need to track VAT if I am not VAT registered?

You do not need to charge or file VAT if your turnover is below £90,000 (2026 threshold). Noting the VAT on receipts is good practice however, especially if you are approaching the threshold or your accountant requests it.

What records does HMRC require a sole trader to keep?

HMRC requires receipts and invoices for all business purchases, bank statements showing business transactions, records of any cash transactions, and mileage logs if you are claiming business travel. Digital copies are fully acceptable.

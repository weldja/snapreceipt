How to track business expenses for UK Self Assessment (the easy way)

April 2026  ·  6 min read  ·  By SnapReceipt

Every January, thousands of UK freelancers and sole traders go through the same ritual: hunting through a shoebox of crumpled receipts, scrolling through months of bank statements, and trying to remember what that £43 payment to Amazon was actually for.

It doesn't have to be like this. With a simple system you can stick to throughout the year, your Self Assessment tax return becomes a 30-minute job instead of a two-day ordeal.

This guide covers everything you need to know — what counts as a business expense, how HMRC expects you to keep records, and the easiest way to stay on top of it all without spending hours on admin.

What counts as a business expense?

HMRC allows you to deduct costs that are incurred "wholly and exclusively" for business purposes. In plain English: if you spent the money because of your work, you can probably claim it.

Common deductible expenses for freelancers and sole traders include:

  • Travel — fuel, train tickets, parking, business mileage (not commuting to a regular workplace)
  • Equipment — laptops, phones, cameras, tools — anything you use for work
  • Software and subscriptions — accounting tools, design software, cloud storage, project management apps
  • Office costs — stationery, printer ink, postage, a proportion of your home broadband if you work from home
  • Professional fees — accountants, solicitors, professional memberships, trade subscriptions
  • Marketing — website costs, advertising, business cards
  • Meals with clients — meals where the primary purpose is a business meeting (keep notes on who you met and why)
Important: If an expense is partly personal and partly business — like a phone you use for both — you can only claim the business portion. HMRC expects you to make a reasonable estimate.

What records does HMRC require you to keep?

As a sole trader, HMRC requires you to keep records of all your business income and expenses for at least 5 years after the Self Assessment deadline for that tax year. So for the 2025/26 tax year (ending 5 April 2026), you need to keep records until at least 31 January 2032.

Your records should include:

  • All receipts and invoices for business purchases
  • Bank statements showing business transactions
  • Records of any cash transactions
  • Mileage logs if you're claiming business travel

HMRC accepts digital records — you don't need to keep paper receipts if you have a clear digital copy. This is where a receipt scanning app becomes particularly useful.

💡 Tip: Always add a note explaining the business purpose — especially for meals and entertainment. "Lunch with client" is not enough; "Lunch with Sarah Jones from Acme Ltd to discuss Q2 project" is much better if HMRC ever asks.

The problem with most expense tracking systems

Most freelancers fall into one of two traps:

The shoebox approach: Keep all receipts in a drawer or bag, then sort them at tax return time. This works until you lose a receipt, can't read a faded thermal printout, or forget what something was for six months later.

The spreadsheet approach: Manually type every receipt into a spreadsheet. Accurate, but time-consuming enough that most people fall behind and end up doing it in bulk — which is the same problem as the shoebox, just with extra steps.

The fundamental issue with both approaches is that they rely on you doing admin at a time that isn't the moment the expense happens. The further you get from the moment of purchase, the harder it is to remember the details.

A better system: log it when it happens

The most effective expense tracking system is one you actually use consistently. That means it needs to be fast enough that you'll do it on the spot — at the restaurant, at the petrol station, leaving a client meeting.

Here's what works:

  1. Scan or photograph every receipt immediately — before it goes in your pocket or bag
  2. Categorise it on the spot — Travel, Meals, Software etc. Takes two seconds
  3. Add a one-line note if it's not obvious — "Client lunch — Sarah Jones" or "Home broadband — 50% business use"
  4. Export to CSV at the end of each quarter — send to your accountant or use directly for your tax return

That's the whole system. The critical step is the first one — capturing the receipt at the point of purchase. Everything else follows naturally.

What to do with receipts you've already missed

If you're starting mid-year with a backlog of receipts, don't panic. Work backwards through your bank and credit card statements — most business expenses will be visible there. For any transactions you can't match to a receipt, check your email inbox for digital receipts and order confirmations.

Going forward, the key is consistency. Even capturing 80% of your receipts is vastly better than 0%, and once the habit is established it becomes automatic.

VAT — do you need to worry about it?

If your taxable turnover is below the VAT registration threshold (£90,000 as of 2026), you don't need to register for VAT and don't charge it to clients. However, you can still note the VAT included in your receipts — this is useful context and some accountants want it.

If you are VAT-registered, you'll need to keep a VAT account and file VAT returns separately from your Self Assessment. In this case, a more detailed record-keeping system (and probably accounting software) is worth the investment.

The start of a new tax year is the best time to get organised

The UK tax year runs from 6 April to 5 April. The new year that started on 6 April is a genuinely good moment to put a system in place — you have a clean slate, no backlog, and 9 months before Self Assessment deadline pressure kicks in.

If you put a 60-second receipt scanning habit in place now, by the time January comes around you'll have a complete, categorised record of every business expense — ready to hand to your accountant or enter directly into your tax return.

The maths: If you spend 2 minutes scanning receipts after each purchase, and you have 5 business purchases a week, that's 10 minutes a week. Over a tax year, that's about 8 hours of admin — versus the 2-day January scramble most freelancers experience.

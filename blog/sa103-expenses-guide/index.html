SA103 expenses: complete guide to every expense box (2026/27)

May 2026 · 12 min read · By SnapReceipt

ⓘ Disclaimer: This guide is for general information only and does not constitute tax advice. Tax rules can change and individual circumstances vary. If you are uncertain about your own position, speak to a qualified accountant or tax adviser.

Every business expense you claim as a sole trader reduces your taxable profit — and your tax bill. But HMRC does not let you claim just anything. The rules are organised around SA103: the supplementary page of your Self Assessment where business expenses are reported.

Understanding what goes in each SA103 box matters more than ever. From April 2026, Making Tax Digital requires sole traders with income over £50,000 to categorise expenses throughout the year rather than at year end. Get the boxes right as you go, and your quarterly MTD updates are straightforward. Get them wrong, and you face corrections, missed claims, and potential compliance questions.

This guide covers every SA103 expense category — what qualifies, what HMRC specifically disallows, and the edge cases that catch people out.

📌 Quick answer: SA103 expense categories map to the boxes HMRC uses on your Self Assessment return and MTD quarterly updates. The allowable test for every category is the same: the expense must be incurred wholly and exclusively for the purposes of your trade.

What is SA103?

SA103 is the self-employment supplementary page that sole traders attach to their SA100 Self Assessment tax return. It is where you report your trading income and claim your allowable business expenses. The result — income minus allowable expenses — is your taxable profit, which feeds into your income tax and Class 4 National Insurance calculation.

There are two versions:

SA103S — Short form
For most sole traders
Use this if your annual turnover is under £85,000 and your affairs are straightforward. SA103S has fewer, simplified expense categories rather than the full SA103F breakdown.
SA103F — Full form
For higher turnover or complex businesses
Required if your annual turnover is over £85,000, or if you have more complex tax affairs. Has separate boxes for each expense category — numbered 17 to 29 on the actual HMRC form.

💡 MTD note: Under Making Tax Digital for Income Tax, most MTD-compatible software uses the SA103F category structure for quarterly expense updates — regardless of your turnover. So even if you would normally use SA103S, you will likely be categorising expenses by type throughout the year.

The golden rule: wholly and exclusively

Before looking at individual categories, there is one rule that governs every SA103 expense claim. To be allowable, an expense must be incurred wholly and exclusively for the purposes of your trade. This is set out in HMRC’s guidance on self-employed expenses and the Business Income Manual (BIM37000).

Wholly: The cost was incurred only for the business — not partly for private reasons.

Exclusively: The sole purpose of the expense was business. If an expense has a dual purpose that cannot be cleanly separated, it is disallowed in full.

⚠️ The dual purpose trap: This is why you cannot claim a suit on your tax return even if you only wear it to client meetings. HMRC’s position, established in Mallalieu v Drummond [1983], is that clothing also provides warmth and decency — a personal benefit — giving it a dual purpose. The same logic applies to gym membership, most food and drink, and commuting costs.

Where costs genuinely split between business and personal use — a mobile phone bill, home broadband — you can claim the business proportion. You need to be able to justify the split if HMRC asks.

📄 Cash basis vs accruals basis: Most sole traders with turnover under £150,000 use the cash basis by default, meaning income and expenses are recorded when money actually changes hands. This guide assumes cash basis unless stated. If you use traditional accruals accounting, some rules — particularly around stock — differ.

SA103 expense categories: what goes in each one

The category names below are used in most MTD-compatible software and match the SA103F structure. On the actual SA103F form, these map to boxes numbered 17 to 29. SA103S uses a simplified set of the same categories.

Office costs

Day-to-day costs of running your business, including home office expenses.

Stationery & printer ink Postage Printing Software subscriptions (wholly business) Home office flat rate or proportional costs
Personal purchases Capital assets expected to last several years (use capital allowances)

Home office: HMRC flat rate — £10/month (25–50 hrs worked), £18/month (51–100 hrs), £26/month (101+ hrs). These rates have not been updated since 2013 and may be significantly lower than the proportional method for many home workers. Alternatively, claim the business proportion of actual household bills — often a larger, equally valid claim.

Travel & vehicles

Business travel costs, including mileage if you use your own vehicle.

Mileage — 45p/mile (first 10,000) Mileage — 25p/mile (above 10,000) Train, bus, taxi for business Parking on business journeys Congestion charge Hotel for overnight business travel
Commuting to a fixed, regular workplace Personal journeys Daily lunch at your desk

If you use the flat mileage rate for a vehicle, you cannot also claim fuel, insurance, or MOT costs for the same vehicle. Choose one method per vehicle and stick with it for the life of that vehicle. See our mileage rates guide for the full rules.

Clothing

Only a narrow category of work clothing qualifies — this is one of HMRC’s most frequently applied disallowances.

Protective clothing (hard hat, hi-vis, safety boots) Branded uniforms Costumes (performers, entertainers)
Suits & smart clothing Everyday work attire Shoes (unless safety footwear)

The dual purpose rule applies strictly here. A plain suit worn only to client meetings is not allowable — it provides warmth and decency as well as a professional appearance. This was established in Mallalieu v Drummond [1983].

Staff costs

Costs of paying people who work for your business.

Wages & salaries paid to employees Employer National Insurance contributions Subcontractor payments (CIS) Agency staff costs Staff pension contributions
Your own drawings as sole trader Payments to family for personal tasks

As a sole trader you cannot pay yourself a salary and claim it as an expense. Payments to family members can be allowable, but only where the work is genuine, the rate is commercial, and the payment is actually made.

Stock & materials

The cost of goods or materials used in your trade.

Raw materials Goods bought for resale Packaging materials Consumables used in the work Building materials (tradespeople)
Personal purchases Capital assets expected to last several years

Cash basis: Under the cash basis (used by most sole traders by default), stock is expensed when you pay for it — not when you sell it. Under the accruals basis, only goods sold or consumed in the tax year are expensed, and unsold closing stock is carried forward. If you are unsure which basis you use, it is most likely cash basis.

Financial charges

Costs of using financial services and insuring the business.

Business bank account fees Business loan interest Professional indemnity insurance Public liability insurance Business credit card fees
Personal bank charges Personal insurance (life, home, car) Loan capital repayments (interest only is allowable)
Advertising & marketing

Costs of promoting your business and finding customers.

Google Ads, Meta Ads Website hosting & domain names Design fees for marketing materials Print advertising Social media tools & scheduling Business cards & leaflets
Client entertainment (meals, drinks, events) Client gifts (strict conditions apply)

Client entertainment is specifically disallowed, with very limited exceptions. Client gifts are allowable only if they: carry a conspicuous advertisement for your business, are not food, drink, tobacco, or a voucher, and cost under £50 per recipient per year. If in doubt, treat client gifts as not allowable.

Phone & internet

Communications costs used for business purposes.

Business-only mobile phone (100%) Business proportion of personal phone bill Business proportion of home broadband Second SIM used solely for business VoIP and business calling services
Full personal phone bill if mixed use Personal streaming or entertainment services

If your phone is mixed use, estimate the business proportion and document your reasoning. Review one month’s usage to establish a percentage, then apply it consistently throughout the year.

Professional fees

Costs of expert services and professional development directly related to your current trade.

Accountant & bookkeeper fees Solicitor fees (business matters) Professional memberships & subscriptions Training that maintains or updates existing skills Trade association fees
Training that qualifies you for a new profession Personal legal costs Fines and penalties

The distinction on training is important: a course that develops skills you already use in your current trade is generally allowable. A course that qualifies you for a different profession is generally not — even if the new skills indirectly benefit your business. If uncertain, seek professional advice.

Repairs & renewals

The cost of maintaining and repairing business equipment or premises — keeping them in working order, not improving them.

Repairing business tools or equipment Routine maintenance of business premises Like-for-like replacement of a broken item
Improvements (these are capital in nature) Repairs to personal property Initial repairs when first acquiring a business asset

The distinction between repair (allowable) and improvement (capital) can be subtle. Repainting a business room is a repair; converting a room into an office is an improvement. If in doubt, treat it as capital and claim via capital allowances.

Other allowable expenses

Business expenses that do not fit neatly into the above categories but still pass the wholly and exclusively test.

Trade magazine subscriptions Business-related books & reference materials Cleaning business premises Business-related events & exhibitions
Personal items Anything with a private benefit

The same wholly and exclusively rule applies. If HMRC queries a claim in this box, you must be able to show the expense was for business purposes only and provide a supporting receipt or invoice.

What you cannot claim on SA103

Several expense types come up repeatedly in HMRC compliance checks. These are worth knowing explicitly because they are commonly claimed and commonly disallowed.

  • Client entertainment. Meals, drinks, hospitality, or events with clients are specifically disallowed under HMRC rules, with very limited exceptions. This is one of the adjustments HMRC makes most frequently in self-employment enquiries — the business purpose of the conversation makes no difference to the disallowance.
  • Everyday clothing. A suit, smart shoes, or business attire is not allowable even if worn exclusively for work. HMRC’s position, established in Mallalieu v Drummond [1983], is that clothing provides warmth and decency — a personal benefit — giving it a dual purpose. Only protective clothing, safety equipment, and branded uniforms qualify.
  • Commuting. Travel between your home and a fixed, regular workplace is generally not a business expense. If you genuinely work from home and travel to a client’s premises or a temporary work location, those journeys are typically claimable. If you attend the same office regularly, those journeys are commuting regardless of your employment status.
  • Capital assets. Equipment, machinery, or assets expected to last several years are capital in nature and claimed via capital allowances (Annual Investment Allowance), not as revenue expenses on SA103. The question to ask: will this item be consumed in the work, or will it last and be used across multiple tax years?
  • Your own drawings. As a sole trader, money you take from the business for personal use is not an allowable expense. Your taxable profit is calculated before any drawings.
  • Fines and penalties. HMRC fines, parking tickets, and regulatory penalties are not allowable, even if incurred in the course of running the business.

How to fill in SA103 expenses

On the SA103F, expenses are entered in the individual boxes numbered 17 to 29 on the form. On the SA103S, you use a simplified set of the same categories. In both cases you enter totals — not individual receipts. The receipts themselves are your supporting evidence, which HMRC can request during a compliance check.

Wages, salaries and other staff costsEmployee wages, employer NIC, subcontractor payments
Car, van and travel expensesTotal mileage claim (miles × rate) plus other business travel costs
Rent, rates, power and insurance costsBusiness premises costs, or home office proportion of household bills
Repairs and renewals of property and equipmentMaintenance and like-for-like repairs of business assets
Phone, fax, stationery and other office costsPhone, internet, postage, software, office supplies

Interest and bank and credit card chargesBusiness bank fees, loan interest, business insurance premiums
Accountancy, legal and other professional feesAccountant, solicitor, membership fees, CPD training
Other business expensesAny allowable business costs not covered by the boxes above

Keep all receipts and invoices for at least 5 years after the 31 January submission deadline for the relevant tax year — for example, records for the 2025/26 tax year should be kept until at least January 2032.

Frequently asked questions about SA103 expenses

What is SA103 on a Self Assessment tax return?

SA103 is the self-employment supplementary page that sole traders attach to their SA100 Self Assessment tax return. It is where you report business income and claim allowable expenses. SA103S is the simplified version for turnover under £85,000; SA103F is the full version with separate boxes numbered 17 to 29.

What expenses can I claim on SA103?

Any expense incurred wholly and exclusively for your trade. The main categories are: office costs, travel and vehicle costs, clothing (protective or uniform only), staff costs, cost of goods for resale, financial charges, advertising and marketing, phone and internet, professional fees, repairs and renewals, and other allowable expenses.

Can I claim clothing on SA103?

Only protective clothing, safety equipment, and branded uniforms. Ordinary clothing — suits, smart shoes, general workwear — is not claimable even if worn exclusively for work. HMRC’s view is that such clothing also provides warmth and decency, making it dual-purpose and therefore disallowed.

Can I claim meals and client entertainment on SA103?

Client entertainment — meals, drinks, hospitality — is specifically disallowed, with very limited exceptions. It is one of HMRC’s most common compliance adjustments. Subsistence when travelling for business away from your normal base is generally allowable, particularly when the journey is significantly outside your normal routine or involves an overnight stay.

What is the difference between SA103S and SA103F?

SA103S (short) is for sole traders with turnover under £85,000 — it has fewer, simplified expense categories. SA103F (full) is required for turnover over £85,000 and has separate boxes numbered 17 to 29. Under Making Tax Digital, most software uses the SA103F category structure regardless of turnover.

Do SA103 expense categories matter for Making Tax Digital?

Yes. Most MTD-compatible software categorises expenses using the SA103 structure, meaning correct categorisation throughout the year feeds directly into your quarterly HMRC submissions. It is no longer a year-end tidy-up — it needs to happen as you spend.

Can I claim my mobile phone on SA103?

Yes, under phone and internet costs. A phone used solely for business is 100% claimable. If you use one phone for both business and personal purposes, claim the business proportion only. A second phone used exclusively for business can be claimed in full.

Can I claim software subscriptions on SA103?

Yes, under office costs. Software used wholly for business — accounting tools, design apps, project management, cloud storage — is fully claimable. If software is used for both business and personal purposes, only the business proportion is allowable.

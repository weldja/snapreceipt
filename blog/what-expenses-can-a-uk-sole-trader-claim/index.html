What expenses can a UK sole trader claim?

April 2026  ·  8 min read  ·  By SnapReceipt

One of the most common questions from new freelancers and sole traders is: what can I actually claim as a business expense? Get it right and you can significantly reduce your tax bill. Get it wrong and you risk an HMRC enquiry.

This guide covers every major expense category, what's allowable, what's not, and how to handle the grey areas that trip most people up.

The golden rule: HMRC allows you to deduct expenses that are incurred "wholly and exclusively" for business purposes. If something has a personal element, you can only claim the business portion.

Travel and transport

Travel is one of the most commonly claimed — and most commonly misclaimed — expense categories.

You can claim:

  • Fuel for business journeys (not commuting to a regular workplace)
  • Train, bus, and coach tickets for business travel
  • Taxi and rideshare fares for business journeys
  • Parking charges during business trips
  • Flights for business travel
  • Congestion charge and tolls on business routes
  • Business mileage if you use your own car (HMRC approved rate: 45p per mile for the first 10,000 miles, 25p after)

You cannot claim:

  • Commuting costs — travel from home to your regular place of work
  • Fines and penalties (parking tickets, speeding fines)
  • Personal travel even if combined with a business trip
💡 Tip: If you work from home with no fixed office, HMRC may allow more travel claims as most journeys could be considered business-related. Keep a mileage log — date, destination, purpose, and miles driven — for any car travel you claim.

Equipment and tools

Physical items you buy to use in your business can generally be claimed as capital allowances or, for smaller purchases, as revenue expenses.

You can claim:

  • Computers, laptops, tablets used for work
  • Phones (the business-use proportion)
  • Cameras, microphones, and recording equipment
  • Trade tools and specialist equipment
  • Office furniture used exclusively for work
  • Printers, scanners, and peripherals

For equipment used partly personally and partly for business — like a phone — you can only claim the business use percentage. Keep notes on how you've calculated this.

Software and digital subscriptions

This is one of the clearest categories — software you use for work is fully deductible.

You can claim:

  • Accounting and invoicing software
  • Design, editing, or creative software
  • Project management tools
  • Cloud storage (business use)
  • Communication tools like Slack or Zoom
  • Website hosting and domain names
  • Security software and VPNs
  • AI tools used for work

If a subscription is used for both work and personal purposes — like a streaming service you occasionally use for research — you can only claim the business proportion, and you'd need to justify this if asked.

Office costs

You can claim:

  • Stationery and office supplies
  • Printer ink and toner
  • Postage and courier costs
  • Professional journals and trade publications
  • Books and reference materials related to your work
  • Rented office or desk space

Working from home

If you work from home, you can claim a proportion of your household costs as a business expense. HMRC offers two methods:

Flat rate (simplified): HMRC allows a fixed amount per month based on how many hours you work from home:

  • 25–50 hours per month: £10/month
  • 51–100 hours per month: £18/month
  • 101+ hours per month: £26/month

Actual costs (more work, potentially more money): Calculate the business proportion of your mortgage interest or rent, council tax, utilities, and broadband based on the number of rooms used for work and the proportion of time spent working.

💡 Tip: The flat rate is much simpler to calculate and defend if HMRC asks questions. Unless you have a large dedicated office space, it's often not worth the complexity of calculating actual costs.

Meals and entertaining

This is one of the most misunderstood areas — and one where people often overclaim.

You can claim:

  • Meals during overnight business trips away from home
  • Meals with clients where the primary purpose is business (with notes on who you met and what was discussed)
  • Reasonable subsistence costs on day trips away from your normal work location

You cannot claim:

  • Your everyday lunch or coffee — even if you're working
  • Entertainment that has no clear business purpose
  • Alcohol at client meals is a grey area — keep it reasonable and document the business purpose
Common mistake: Claiming your daily coffee or lunch as a business expense. HMRC does not allow this — food and drink for yourself while working is considered a personal expense unless you are away from home on a business trip.

Marketing and advertising

You can claim:

  • Website design and development costs
  • Online advertising (Google Ads, social media ads)
  • Business cards and printed marketing materials
  • Photography for business use
  • PR and marketing agency fees
  • Samples given to potential customers

Professional fees and training

You can claim:

  • Accountant and bookkeeper fees
  • Solicitor fees for business matters
  • Professional membership fees relevant to your work
  • Training courses directly related to your current business (not for a new career)
  • Books and courses for professional development in your field

You cannot claim:

  • Training to enter a new profession or career
  • Personal development courses not directly related to your current work

Insurance

You can claim:

  • Professional indemnity insurance
  • Public liability insurance
  • Business contents insurance
  • Vehicle insurance (business proportion, if used for work)

Bank charges and financial costs

You can claim:

  • Business bank account fees
  • Interest on business loans
  • Card transaction fees (e.g. Stripe, PayPal fees)
  • Accountancy software subscriptions

How to keep records HMRC will accept

Knowing what you can claim is only half the battle — you also need to keep the evidence. HMRC can ask to see your records up to 5 years after the Self Assessment deadline for that tax year, so keeping organised records is essential.

For each expense you claim, you should have:

  • A receipt or invoice showing the amount, date, and supplier
  • A note of the business purpose (especially for meals and travel)
  • Bank or card statement showing the payment was made

HMRC accepts digital records — you don't need to keep paper receipts. A clear photo of a receipt is sufficient, which is why scanning receipts as you go is so much better than keeping a shoebox.

The bottom line: If you can explain what it was for, who it was with, and why it was necessary for your business, you're probably fine. If you can't, don't claim it.

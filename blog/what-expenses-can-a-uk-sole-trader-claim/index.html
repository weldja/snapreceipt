HMRC allowable expenses for UK sole traders (2026): complete guide

April 2026·12 min read·By SnapReceipt

One of the most common questions from new freelancers and sole traders is: what can I actually claim as a business expense? Get it right and you can significantly reduce your HMRC tax bill. Get it wrong and you risk an enquiry.

This guide covers every major HMRC allowable expense category for sole traders, what's allowable, what's not, how to handle the grey areas — and the worked tax-saving example that shows exactly how much these deductions are worth.

💰 Why this matters: If you earn £45,000 and claim £9,000 in allowable expenses, you pay tax on £36,000 — not £45,000. At 20% basic rate, that's £1,800 saved in Income Tax alone, plus savings on National Insurance.

The HMRC "wholly and exclusively" rule explained

Every HMRC allowable expense for sole traders must pass one test: the cost was incurred wholly and exclusively for the purposes of the business. This is defined in HMRC Business Income Manual BIM37000.

In practice this means:

  • If an expense is purely for business — claim it in full
  • If it has a personal element that can be separated (like a phone bill) — claim the business proportion only
  • If it has a personal element that cannot be separated (like everyday clothing) — you cannot claim any of it

💡 Common mistake: Claiming 100% of mixed-use items. If your laptop is 70% business and 30% personal, claim 70%. HMRC can deny the entire expense if you overclaim and then ask for justification.

Travel and transport expenses HMRC sole traders can claim

Travel is one of the most commonly claimed — and most commonly misclaimed — expense categories.

You can claim:

  • Business mileage at HMRC approved rates (45p/mile first 10,000 miles, 25p after — for cars and vans)
  • Train, bus, coach and taxi fares for business journeys
  • Flights for business travel
  • Parking charges during business trips
  • Congestion charge and road tolls on business routes

You cannot claim:

  • Commuting — travel from home to your regular place of work
  • Fines and penalties (parking tickets, speeding fines)
  • Personal travel even if combined with a business trip

💡 If you work from home with no fixed office, HMRC may allow more travel claims as most journeys could be considered business-related. Keep a mileage log for every car journey — date, start point, destination, purpose and miles — as HMRC can and does reject claims without one. Equipment, tools and technology: what can a sole trader claim?

Physical items bought for use in your business can generally be claimed as capital allowances or, for smaller purchases, as revenue expenses (deducted in full in the year of purchase).

You can claim:

  • Computers, laptops and tablets used for work
  • Phones — the business-use proportion
  • Cameras, microphones and recording equipment
  • Trade tools and specialist equipment
  • Office furniture used exclusively for work
  • Printers, scanners and peripherals

For equipment used partly personally and partly for business, you can only claim the business-use percentage. Keep a brief note explaining how you calculated this split.

📌 Capital allowances note: Equipment costing over £1,000 that will last more than a year is usually treated as a capital asset rather than a revenue expense. Most sole traders can claim the full cost in year one under the Annual Investment Allowance (AIA), which covers up to £1,000,000 of qualifying equipment per year.

Software and digital subscriptions

This is one of the clearest categories — software you use for work is fully deductible as an HMRC allowable expense.

You can claim:

  • Accounting and invoicing software
  • Design, editing or creative software
  • Project management tools
  • Cloud storage for business use
  • Communication tools (Slack, Zoom, Teams)
  • Website hosting and domain names
  • Security software and VPNs
  • AI tools used for work

If a subscription is used for both work and personal purposes — like a streaming service you occasionally use for research — claim only the business proportion and be able to justify it if asked.

Working from home allowance UK sole trader 2026 (flat rate vs actual costs)

If you work from home, you can claim a proportion of your household costs. HMRC offers two methods:

Flat rate (simplified expenses)
25–50 hours/month: £10/month
51–100 hours/month: £18/month
101+ hours/month: £26/month

Simpler to calculate and defend. Best for most sole traders.
Actual costs (proportion method)
Calculate business % of rent/mortgage interest, council tax, utilities and broadband based on rooms used and hours worked.

More paperwork. Can be worth it with a large dedicated workspace.

📊 Worked example (actual costs): Annual household bills of £14,400. You use 1 room out of 5 exclusively for business, 5 days a week. Claim: £14,400 × 20% = £2,880. At 20% tax, that saves £576 per year — significantly more than the flat rate of £312 (26 × 12).

Use the HMRC simplified expenses checker to compare both methods for your situation.

Can sole traders claim meals as a business expense? HMRC rules

This is one of the most misunderstood areas — and where people most commonly overclaim.

You can claim:

  • Meals during overnight business trips away from home
  • Client meals where the primary purpose is business (document who you met and what was discussed)
  • Reasonable subsistence on day trips away from your normal work location

You cannot claim:

  • Your everyday lunch or coffee — even if you are working
  • Entertainment that has no clear, documented business purpose
  • Client entertainment generally — HMRC classifies this as "entertaining" rather than a business expense, and disallows it

⚠️ Most common mistake: Claiming daily coffee or lunch as a business expense. HMRC does not allow this — food and drink for yourself while working is personal subsistence. This is HMRC's most common adjustment reason in sole trader enquiries.

Office costs, marketing and professional fees

Office costs you can claim:

  • Stationery and office supplies
  • Printer ink and toner
  • Postage and courier costs
  • Professional journals and trade publications
  • Books and reference materials related to your work
  • Rented office or desk space

Marketing you can claim:

  • Website design and development costs
  • Online advertising (Google Ads, social media ads)
  • Business cards and printed marketing materials
  • Photography for business use
  • PR and marketing agency fees

Professional fees you can claim:

  • Accountant and bookkeeper fees
  • Solicitor fees for business matters
  • Professional membership fees relevant to your work
  • Training courses directly related to your current business activity

You cannot claim: Training to enter a new profession or change career. The training must relate to your existing business, not a planned future one.

Insurance, bank charges and financial costs

You can claim:

  • Professional indemnity insurance
  • Public liability insurance
  • Business contents insurance
  • Business bank account fees
  • Interest on business loans (not capital repayments)
  • Payment processing fees (Stripe, PayPal, SumUp transaction fees)

HMRC allowable expenses and Making Tax Digital (MTD) 2026

From April 2026, sole traders and landlords with income over £50,000 must keep digital records and submit quarterly updates to HMRC under Making Tax Digital for Income Tax Self Assessment (MTD ITSA). The threshold drops to £30,000 in April 2027.

What this means for expense tracking:

  • Paper records are no longer sufficient for those within MTD — all expense records must be digital
  • You must submit quarterly summaries of income and expenses to HMRC (not just an annual return)
  • Receipts must be captured and stored digitally, not just summarised at year end

If you are approaching the £50,000 threshold, moving to digital record-keeping now — rather than waiting until you are required — means no disruption when MTD applies to you. See HMRC's full MTD for Income Tax guidance →

How to keep HMRC-compliant expense records for Self Assessment

Knowing what you can claim is only half the battle — you also need to keep evidence. HMRC can ask to see your records up to 5 years after the Self Assessment deadline, so organised records are essential.

For each expense you claim, you should have:

  • A receipt or invoice showing the amount, date and supplier
  • A note of the business purpose (especially for meals, travel and mixed-use items)
  • A bank or card statement showing the payment was made

HMRC accepts digital records — a clear photo of a receipt is sufficient. See our guide to tracking and logging expenses throughout the year →

💡 The bottom line: If you can explain what it was for, who it was with, and why it was necessary for your business, you're probably fine. If you can't justify it, don't claim it.

Frequently asked questions about sole trader expenses

What is the HMRC "wholly and exclusively" rule for sole traders?

HMRC only allows you to deduct costs incurred wholly and exclusively for business purposes. If an expense has a personal element that cannot be separated, the whole expense is disallowed. Where you can identify a business proportion (such as a phone bill), you claim only that proportion. See HMRC BIM37000 for the full definition.

Can I claim my home office as a sole trader expense?

Yes. HMRC offers a simplified flat rate of £10–£26 per month depending on hours worked from home. Alternatively, you can claim a proportion of your actual household costs based on the number of rooms used and hours worked. For most sole traders the flat rate is simpler and easier to defend.

Can a sole trader claim food and drink as a business expense?

Only in limited circumstances — meals during overnight business trips away from home, and client lunches with a clearly documented business purpose. Your everyday lunch or coffee while working is personal subsistence and not allowable, even if you're working at a café.

Can I claim my mobile phone as a sole trader business expense?

Yes, but only the business-use proportion. If you use your phone 60% for work, claim 60% of the monthly bill. Keep a brief note of how you calculated the percentage in case HMRC asks. A phone on a business-only contract can be claimed in full.

What expenses can a sole trader NOT claim?

Disallowable expenses include: commuting to a regular workplace, everyday clothing (even if worn only for work), client entertainment, personal food and drink, fines and penalties, drawings from the business, pension contributions (claimed separately as personal relief), and capital repayments on loans (though interest is allowable).

UK Self Assessment deadlines and penalties 2026/27: complete guide for freelancers

April 2026 · 11 min read · By SnapReceipt

Miss the UK Self Assessment deadline and HMRC starts adding penalties from day one — regardless of whether you owe any tax. For UK freelancers and sole traders, understanding the key dates for 2026/27, how the penalty structure works, and what to do if you fall behind can save you hundreds of pounds and considerable stress.

This guide covers every Self Assessment deadline for the 2025/26 tax year, the full penalty ladder, payments on account (the trap that catches most new sole traders), and how to appeal if you do get penalised.

⚠️ Important note on MTD for 2026/27: From April 2026, sole traders and landlords with income over £50,000 are now required to use Making Tax Digital for Income Tax (MTD ITSA) — replacing traditional Self Assessment with quarterly digital submissions. If you earn over £50,000, your deadlines are different from those described here. See HMRC's MTD ITSA guidance →

All the key Self Assessment dates for 2026/27

The following dates apply to the 2025/26 tax year (6 April 2025 to 5 April 2026) — the year you'll be reporting on and paying tax for in 2026/27.

5 October 2026
Register for Self Assessment
If you became self-employed in the 2025/26 tax year and haven't yet registered, you must do so by 5 October 2026. Missing this date triggers a penalty. Register at gov.uk.
31 October 2026
Paper tax return deadline
If you are filing a paper Self Assessment return for 2025/26, it must reach HMRC by 31 October 2026. Most freelancers file online — in which case this date doesn't apply to you.
31 January 2027
Online tax return AND payment deadline — the big one
Your 2025/26 online Self Assessment return must be filed AND any tax owed must be paid by midnight on 31 January 2027. This is also the deadline for your first payment on account towards 2026/27 (see below). Miss this and penalties begin immediately.
31 July 2027
Second payment on account
Your second payment on account towards the 2026/27 tax year is due by 31 July 2027. If you're new to Self Assessment this may catch you by surprise — see the payments on account section below.

💡 Set calendar reminders now for 31 October 2026 and 31 January 2027. Add them as recurring annual events — these dates stay the same every year.

⚠️ You get a penalty even if you owe £0 in tax. The £100 day-one penalty applies to everyone who files late, regardless of their tax bill. If you earned below the personal allowance (£12,570 in 2025/26), you still need to file on time — or appeal with a reasonable excuse.

Late payment penalties and interest (separate from filing penalties)

Filing penalties and payment penalties are two separate systems. Even if you file on time, you'll face payment penalties if you don't pay what you owe by 31 January 2027:

If you cannot pay by the deadline, contact HMRC before 31 January 2027 to set up a Time to Pay arrangement. Interest will still accrue, but the late payment surcharges (the 5% penalties) can sometimes be avoided if you contact HMRC proactively. Call the Self Assessment helpline on 0300 200 3310.

Payments on account explained — the cash-flow trap no one warns you about

Payments on account are one of the biggest financial surprises for new sole traders. If your Self Assessment tax bill exceeds £1,000 and less than 80% of your income was taxed at source through PAYE, HMRC requires you to make advance payments towards the following year's bill.

Each payment on account is 50% of your previous year's tax bill, paid twice:

31 Jan 2027
50%
First payment on account for 2026/27 tax year (paid alongside your 2025/26 bill)
31 Jul 2027
50%
Second payment on account for 2026/27 tax year
31 Jan 2028
Balance
Balancing payment once actual 2026/27 bill is known, minus payments already made

⚠️ The January double-payment shock: If your 2025/26 tax bill is £3,000, on 31 January 2027 you will pay £3,000 (your 2025/26 bill) plus £1,500 (first payment on account for 2026/27) = £4,500 in a single payment. This catches many new sole traders off guard. Budget for it from the start of each tax year.

If your income will be lower next year, you can apply to reduce your payments on account through your HMRC online account. Be cautious — if you reduce them too much and your actual bill is higher, HMRC charges interest on the underpayment.

How to appeal a Self Assessment penalty

If you receive a late-filing or late-payment penalty and believe you have a reasonable excuse, you can appeal. You must appeal within 30 days of receiving the penalty notice.

What counts as a reasonable excuse?

  • Serious or life-threatening illness (yours or a close family member's)
  • Bereavement of a close family member shortly before the deadline
  • Unexpected HMRC system failure preventing online filing
  • A fire, flood or natural disaster affecting your records
  • Postal delays outside your control (paper returns)

What does NOT count as a reasonable excuse?

  • Pressure of work or being too busy
  • Not knowing the deadline existed
  • Relying on someone else (an accountant or agent) who failed to file — you are still responsible
  • Lack of information from HMRC
  • Finding the return too difficult or complex

To appeal, use your HMRC online account or write to HMRC with the penalty reference number, the grounds for appeal, and supporting evidence. HMRC will review the appeal and issue a decision — you can escalate to the Tax Tribunal if refused.

Making Tax Digital 2026: how deadlines are changing

For sole traders and landlords with income over £50,000, the traditional annual Self Assessment return is being replaced by Making Tax Digital for Income Tax (MTD ITSA) from April 2026. Instead of one return per year, MTD requires:

  • Quarterly updates — submitted to HMRC every 3 months with a summary of income and expenses
  • A final declaration — replacing the traditional Self Assessment return, submitted by 31 January after the tax year
  • Digital record-keeping — paper records are no longer sufficient

The quarterly submission deadlines for the 2026/27 tax year under MTD are:

The MTD threshold drops to income over £30,000 from April 2027, and eventually to £20,000. Even if you're below the threshold now, adopting digital record-keeping means zero disruption when MTD applies to you.

How to make sure you never miss a Self Assessment deadline again

The best defence against penalties is a system that makes compliance automatic rather than something you have to remember. Here's what works:

  • File early. You can file your Self Assessment return as soon as the tax year ends on 5 April — don't wait until January. Filing in April or May means no deadline stress, any refund arrives sooner, and you have 9 months to plan for the payment.
  • Set aside tax throughout the year. A common rule of thumb is to save 25–30% of every invoice payment into a separate account. When the January bill arrives, the money is already there.
  • Keep records as you go. The January scramble starts with disorganised records. Scan receipts immediately, track mileage at the time, and export your records quarterly. See our guide on how to track business expenses for UK Self Assessment →
  • Know your allowable expenses. Claiming all the expenses you're entitled to reduces your tax bill — and with it your payments on account for the following year. See our complete guide to HMRC allowable expenses for sole traders →
  • Use an accountant for complex situations. If you have multiple income sources, high earnings, or rental income, an accountant typically saves more than their fee.

Frequently asked questions about Self Assessment deadlines and penalties

What is the Self Assessment deadline for 2026/27?

The online Self Assessment deadline for the 2025/26 tax year is 31 January 2027. The paper return deadline is 31 October 2026. Both your return and any tax owed must be submitted/paid by 31 January 2027.

What happens if I miss the Self Assessment deadline?

An automatic £100 penalty applies from day one. If your return is 3 months late, HMRC adds £10 per day for up to 90 days. At 6 months late, a further 5% of tax owed (or £300, whichever is greater) is added. At 12 months, another 5% or £300. Penalties apply even if you owe no tax.

What if I can't pay my Self Assessment bill on time?

Contact HMRC before the deadline to arrange a Time to Pay agreement. Interest accrues from the deadline date, but if you contact HMRC proactively the 5% late payment surcharges may be avoidable. Call 0300 200 3310 before 31 January 2027.

Can HMRC waive a Self Assessment penalty?

Yes, if you have a reasonable excuse — serious illness, bereavement, an unexpected HMRC system failure, or a natural disaster. You must appeal within 30 days of receiving the penalty notice. Work pressure, not knowing the deadline, or an accountant failing to file are not accepted as reasonable excuses.

Do I still get a Self Assessment penalty if I owe no tax?

Yes. The £100 automatic late-filing penalty applies regardless of whether you owe any tax. The only way to avoid it is to file on time, or to successfully appeal with a valid reasonable excuse.

What are payments on account for Self Assessment?

Payments on account are advance payments towards the following year's tax bill, required when your bill exceeds £1,000 and less than 80% was collected at source through PAYE. Each payment is 50% of the previous year's tax bill, due on 31 January and 31 July. On 31 January you often pay the previous year's bill plus the first payment on account — budget for this double payment.

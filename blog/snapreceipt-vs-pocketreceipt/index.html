The scan limit: why it matters more than it looks

PocketReceipt’s free tier allows 10 scans per month. That sounds reasonable — until you count what a working week actually looks like.

A typical sole trader might scan:

  • Fuel receipts (weekly) → 4–5/month
  • Train or parking (a few trips) → 2–4/month
  • Software subscriptions or tools → 2–3/month
  • Materials, supplies, or client meals → varies

You can hit 10 in a quiet fortnight. During a busy quarter — pre-tax return, catching up on a backlog — 10 is gone in a single sitting.

⚠️ The cap problem: Scan limits are especially awkward for sole traders who batch-process receipts monthly or quarterly rather than scanning on the spot. You hit the wall exactly when you most need to catch up.

SnapReceipt currently has no scan limit. You can scan 3 receipts one month and 80 the next — there is no cap.

Where PocketReceipt pulls ahead

Mileage tracking is the clearest gap. PocketReceipt logs journeys with GPS or manual entry, auto-applies HMRC’s 45p/25p rates, and tracks per vehicle. If you drive to clients, sites, or suppliers regularly, mileage is one of the most valuable deductions available — and one of the most commonly missed. See our HMRC mileage rates guide for the full rules.

SnapReceipt does not currently include mileage tracking. If that is central to your expenses, PocketReceipt is the more complete tool right now.

Smart warnings (duplicate detection and capital item flags) are also PocketReceipt-only. These are genuinely useful at tax time — catching a receipt you scanned twice, or flagging an asset that should go through capital allowances rather than as a direct expense.

Accountant sharing matters if your accountant wants to review your records directly. PocketReceipt has a separate dashboard for accountants with a quarterly review workflow. With SnapReceipt, your accountant works from your exported CSV.

Where SnapReceipt pulls ahead

No scan cap is the headline. But the PWA (Progressive Web App) model is also worth understanding. SnapReceipt runs in your browser — no App Store, no Google Play, no waiting for an app review or update. Add it to your home screen from Safari or Chrome and it behaves like a native app. That means:

  • Works on any device with a browser
  • No storage used on your phone for the app itself
  • Instant updates — no waiting for an app store release

✅ No install friction: For sole traders who pick up a new tool on a whim or recommend it to another freelancer, a browser link is a lower barrier than “go to the App Store, search, download, create an account.” You can be scanning your first receipt in under a minute.

What they have in common

Both tools are purpose-built for UK sole traders, not adapted from US or general-purpose tools. Both:

  • Extract VAT, supplier, date, and amount from receipt photos using AI
  • Align expense categories to SA103 — the Self Assessment sole trader expenses form
  • Support HMRC’s position that digital copies of receipts are acceptable business records
  • Are free to start with no credit card required

Neither submits your tax return. Both produce exports you (or your accountant) use to complete Self Assessment or your MTD submissions. For background on how HMRC expects you to keep digital records, see our guide to keeping receipts for Self Assessment.

💡 MTD note: From April 2026, sole traders with income over £50,000 must submit quarterly expense updates to HMRC. Both apps categorise expenses to SA103 throughout the year, which feeds directly into those updates. See our Making Tax Digital guide for the full picture.

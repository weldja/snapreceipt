HMRC mileage rates 2026/27: complete guide for UK sole traders

April 2026 · 10 min read · Written by SnapReceipt

Quick summary: The HMRC Approved Mileage Allowance Payment (AMAP) rates for 2026/27 are 45p per mile for the first 10,000 business miles in a car or van, then 25p per mile above that. Motorcycles: 24p/mile. Bicycles: 20p/mile. These rates have been unchanged since 2011. Most sole traders are better off using the flat rate rather than claiming actual vehicle costs — this guide explains when and why, and what records HMRC expects.

Current HMRC mileage rates (AMAP) 2026/27

HMRC sets flat-rate mileage allowances — known as Approved Mileage Allowance Payments (AMAP) — that sole traders can use to claim the cost of business journeys in their own vehicles. The rates cover all costs of using the vehicle: fuel, depreciation, wear and tear, insurance, and servicing.

These rates have been unchanged since April 2011. The car/van rate has a two-tier structure: the first 10,000 business miles in the tax year are reimbursed at 45p/mile, and every mile above that threshold drops to 25p/mile. The threshold resets to zero at the start of each new tax year (6 April).

📊 Worked example: You drive 8,000 business miles in 2026/27. Your mileage claim is 8,000 × 45p = £3,600 deducted from your taxable profit. At 20% basic-rate tax that saves you £720 in Income Tax — plus reduces your Class 4 National Insurance liability. If you drove 12,000 business miles: (10,000 × 45p) + (2,000 × 25p) = £4,500 + £500 = £5,000 claimable.

Flat rate vs actual vehicle costs: which should you claim?

Sole traders have two options for vehicle expense claims. You can either use the AMAP flat rate, or you can claim the actual costs of running your vehicle (fuel, insurance, servicing, MOT, road tax, depreciation) apportioned to your business-use percentage. You must choose one method and stick to it for the life of the vehicle — you cannot switch between the two approaches for the same vehicle.

In practice, the flat rate wins for most people. It's simpler, requires far less paperwork, and the 45p/mile rate is typically generous enough to exceed actual running costs for most ordinary cars. The actual costs method only tends to produce a larger deduction if you drive a high-value vehicle with expensive insurance and servicing, and you do a significant proportion of business miles.

⚠️ Important: Once you start claiming actual costs for a vehicle, you cannot switch to AMAP rates for that same vehicle. If you're starting out, the flat rate is usually the simpler and safer default — and you can always use the actual costs method on any new vehicle you acquire later.

What counts as a business mile?

Not every journey in your car is claimable — only travel that is wholly and exclusively for business purposes qualifies under HMRC rules.

Journeys you can claim:

  • Travel to a client's premises, site, or office
  • Travelling to a temporary workplace (even if you go regularly, if it's not your permanent base)
  • Driving to collect business supplies or equipment
  • Travelling to a bank, accountant, or business meeting
  • Journeys between two different workplaces (e.g. home office to a studio you rent)

Journeys you cannot claim:

  • Commuting to a fixed, permanent place of work (even if you're self-employed and your client's office has become your regular base)
  • Any personal journeys, even in a vehicle also used for business
  • School runs, shopping, or other private use — even if incidentally on the same route as a business journey

💡 Home-based sole traders: If you genuinely work from home (your home is your business base), then travel from home to a client's site or a meeting is a claimable business journey — because you are travelling from your workplace to another location. This is a significant benefit for freelancers and consultants who have a home office.

What mileage records does HMRC require?

HMRC does not specify an exact format for mileage records, but they do expect a contemporaneous log — meaning records made at the time of the journey, not reconstructed from memory months later. An HMRC enquiry or compliance check can ask for this evidence, and a log made after the fact is treated with far less credibility than one maintained in real time.

For each business journey, record:

  • Date of the journey
  • Start and end point (or description, e.g. "home to client office — Manchester city centre")
  • Purpose of the journey (e.g. "client meeting — Acme Ltd", "delivering goods to customer")
  • Miles driven for that trip
  • Running total of business miles in the tax year (helps you track the 10,000-mile threshold)

Electric and hybrid vehicles

The HMRC AMAP rate of 45p/mile applies to electric vehicles in the same way as petrol and diesel cars. There is currently no separate, higher rate for EVs, even though electricity is now effectively the main "fuel" cost.

This has been a point of debate — the AMAP rate was last updated in 2011, well before mainstream EV adoption, and the Advisory Electricity Rate (AER) that HMRC uses for company car reimbursements is much lower (currently 7p/mile). However, for sole traders using the AMAP flat rate, the full 45p/mile applies regardless of whether the vehicle is electric, hybrid, petrol, or diesel. HMRC's position is that the rate covers all costs, not just energy.

If you drive an EV and use the actual costs method instead, you can claim the actual electricity cost of charging plus other running costs, apportioned to your business-use percentage.

Passenger payments (the extra 5p/mile)

If you carry a fellow employee as a passenger on a business journey, HMRC allows you to claim an additional 5p per mile on top of the standard AMAP rate. For a 50-mile journey carrying a colleague, that's an extra £2.50 claimable.

This only applies when carrying employees — not clients, contractors, or other business contacts. For most sole traders working alone, this rarely comes up, but if you have any employees this is worth tracking separately in your mileage log.

How to claim mileage on your Self Assessment return

Mileage costs go on the SA103 (self-employment) supplementary page of your Self Assessment return, in the Travel and subsistence expenses section. You enter the total monetary value of your mileage claim — not the number of miles. So if you drove 8,000 business miles, you enter £3,600 (8,000 × 45p), not the raw mileage figure.

Steps to calculate what to enter:

  1. Total up your business miles for the tax year from your mileage log
  2. If total is 10,000 or under: multiply by 0.45
  3. If total exceeds 10,000: calculate (10,000 × 0.45) + (excess miles × 0.25)
  4. Enter the resulting pound amount in the Travel and subsistence box

You do not submit your mileage log with your tax return — HMRC processes it without seeing the supporting evidence. However, you must retain that log for at least 6 years from the end of the relevant tax year, in case of a compliance check. This is the same 6-year rule that applies to all business records and receipts.

Common mistakes to avoid

Reconstructing your mileage log at year end. The most common mistake — and the one HMRC is most likely to challenge. A log that appears to have been filled in retrospectively (all in the same handwriting colour, no corrections, suspiciously round numbers) is a red flag. Record journeys as they happen.

Including commuting miles. If you have a regular fixed workplace, travel to that workplace is not claimable. This catches many sole traders who work partly from a client's office on a long-term contract — once a workplace becomes your regular, permanent base, those miles are commuting.

Mixing business and personal mileage. If you use your car for both, keep only the business journeys in your mileage log. The AMAP rate is specifically for business miles — you cannot average across total mileage.

Forgetting the 10,000-mile threshold. The 45p rate only applies to the first 10,000 business miles. Above that it drops to 25p. If you drive significant business mileage and don't track the running total, you may miscalculate your claim at year end.

Switching methods part-way through. You cannot use AMAP rates for part of a year and actual costs for the other part. Pick one method per vehicle and stick with it for the life of that vehicle.

Frequently asked questions

What are the HMRC mileage rates for 2026/27?
For cars and vans: 45p per mile for the first 10,000 business miles, then 25p per mile above that. Motorcycles: 24p/mile (flat rate). Bicycles: 20p/mile (flat rate). These rates have been unchanged since 2011.
Can I claim 45p per mile as a sole trader?
Yes. Sole traders using a private vehicle for business journeys can claim 45p per mile for the first 10,000 business miles in a tax year, and 25p per mile above that. You record the total monetary value on your SA103 Self Assessment return under Travel and subsistence expenses.
Do HMRC mileage rates include fuel?
Yes. The AMAP rate is designed to cover all costs of using the vehicle: fuel, insurance, depreciation, and wear and tear. If you claim AMAP rates, you cannot additionally claim fuel or other running costs for that vehicle.
What records do I need to keep for a mileage claim?
A contemporaneous mileage log with: the date, start and end location, business purpose, and miles driven for each journey. HMRC expects records made at the time — not reconstructed from memory months later. Retain the log for at least 6 years.
Should I claim mileage or actual vehicle costs?
Most sole traders are better off with the AMAP flat rate — it's simpler and the 45p/mile rate is typically generous enough to cover actual costs for ordinary vehicles. Actual costs only makes sense for high-value vehicles with expensive running costs and a high business-use percentage.
Can I claim mileage for an electric car?
Yes — the 45p/mile AMAP rate applies to electric and hybrid vehicles exactly as it does to petrol and diesel cars. There is currently no separate rate for EVs when using the AMAP method.
Can I claim mileage for commuting?
No. Travel between your home and a fixed, permanent place of work is not an allowable expense. However, if you work from home and travel to a client's premises or a temporary worksite, those miles are claimable as business journeys.
What is the passenger rate?
An additional 5p per mile is claimable when you carry a fellow employee as a passenger on a business journey. This only applies to employees — not clients or contractors.
